Summary

Over lockdown it has become apparent that without the usual physical social avenues, I've realised how difficult it can be to connect with people who are wanting to pursue a common hobby. This can be anything from jamming with other musos or organising a group to play D&D. 'Who's Keen?' is a project designed to facilitate the quick organisation of casual groups. The service will have two main 'elements': users and groups.
Users will have a profile which will provide a summary of their group membership history. They will also have a calendar, drawing events from groups.
Groups will allow basic functionality such as IM and photo sharing, potentially voice/video channels & calendar tools.

Motivation

Over the course of the 2020 lockdown, I realised just how much I relied on my physical correspondence with my friends and colleagues. Over the course of a couple of lockdowns I realised that organising meetups or groups for various things were falling through or garnering little attention, often with their announcement being utterly buried in a group chat within a few days, forgotten shortly thereafter. Having a platform for organising groups, projects or even one-off events would theoretically maximise the chances of getting people interested or onboard.

Description

Who’s Keen?’ will provide a simplified, single-purpose space for bringing groups and events into being. Its focus is narrow so that it may allow rich and meaningful features to be implemented into it. This stems from the fact that several services can provide a similar surface experience, but organising groups & events is rarely a primary focus. As such, we have stripped the service back to the most important parts so that these shine on their own. To describe these, it is probably best that I describe the environment from the user perspective.
When a user creates an account, they will have a very basic profile set up where they add a Bio and select a few things they are interested in being involved with. These interests will allow the user to browse a list of public groups based on their interests. The user may also know of friends who have an account, in which case they will add their friends to their friends list. Over time, as more of their friends adopt the platform, they will have what will hopefully be two rich lists of groups: the public list and the friend list.
Let us say that this user is wanting to form a group of their own – this should be a simple task. The group will have a few attributes, such as public/private, a description, and tags. For example, let us say that George wants to play Dungeons & Dragons with his friends. He would create a private group, adding a description of the campaign he is running and how many people he is hoping to have join. Once this is done, he would add a few tags to help the group be visible to those most interested – perhaps D&D and TTRPG. Now that his group is created, a few friends see the posting and join the group. George now creates an event within the group, titled ‘Session Zero’ and schedules it for this Friday from 6:00PM to 9:00PM over video call. Members would ideally confirm their attendance – this would add the event to their calendar.
Let us look at another use case. Ruth is new to Melbourne and wishes to find local musicians to hang out and jam with. After creating her account, she creates a public group and adds the relevant description and tags. She would then set the member controls to only allow people to join after being approved by the admin(s) – Ruth in this instance. Every few days she checks on the group and allows a few people to join. Once the group reaches capacity, she schedules a recurring event to take place every Saturday arvo in her garage studio.
Finally, let us look at Jeremy, who is a friend of George and plays the drums recreationally. He likes the look of the D&D campaign so joins up. He is free on Friday so accepts the invitation to ‘Session Zero’ – this gets added to his calendar. He then sees that Ruth has scheduled a jam session for Saturday but may be helping a friend move. As such, he selects the option ‘may attend’, and finds a feint event added to his calendar.

Tools and Technologies

The platform would involve a few key components: a server to manage the databases (users, groups, events, etc.), a web portal for users to access the platform from virtually any device, and a mobile app to allow easy access to all the platforms primary features on the move. The server would likely be implemented on a cloud platform such as AWS to enable simple scalability and distribution of the services.

Skills Required

The skills required to produce ‘Who’s Keen?’ can be seen by breaking down the components of the platform.
Let us start with the back end. The backbone to the platform is the database holding the user data, group data, event data and the relationships between these (friends, users members of groups, etc.). As such, an understanding of databases and their administration would be an essential skill to have. Having this implemented would also require skills in server administration and an understanding of cloud concepts.
Second, we need to integrate this into two interfaces – the app interface and the web interface. The first would be implemented by somebody with skills in mobile app development, and the latter with skills in web development. There is likely to be a fair bit of cross- over between the implementations from the UX/design perspective and from the database-integration perspective.
Finally, the project will need a plan for marketing to ensure that it’s adopted by enough users for it to reach a ‘critical mass’.
An additional nicety would be to have proper e2e encryption. A method of monetising the platform would be important to keep in mind too – perhaps a premium subscription, advertisements or perhaps enterprise licencing – each of which would need more skilled individuals to implement.

Outcomes

Once fully implemented, the platform would provide people with a great pathway to develop new social groups based around common interests and to more easily organise events. As such, the original problem outlined in the opening would be solved. Once monetised, it may even be properly viable for long-term, large-scale implementation and be profitable.
